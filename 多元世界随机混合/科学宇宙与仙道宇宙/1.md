# Chapter One — The Scripture That Fell from Nowhere

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Dragon Nation, 2036. Longcheng — a sprawling, smog-streaked megacity in central China where forty million people lived stacked on top of each other like circuit boards in a server rack. Neon-lit skyscrapers jutted through the perpetual haze, their facades alive with scrolling holographic advertisements for gene-editing clinics, autonomous delivery drones, and the latest brain-computer interface headsets.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** The Dragon Nation University of Information Industry — DNUII, or as its students affectionately called it, "The Factory" — occupied an absurdly large campus on the city's eastern edge. Twelve research towers, each thirty stories tall, ringed a central artificial lake rumored to contain more fiber-optic cable beneath its bed than actual water. The Robotics and Intelligent Systems building — Tower Seven — was the tallest of them all, and at 11:47 PM on a Tuesday in late October, its seventeenth-floor graduate lab was still lit up like a lantern.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Xin sat at her workstation, three monitors arranged in an arc around her, all displaying different feeds: a real-time simulation of a quadruped robot navigating rubble terrain, a dense wall of Python logs, and a research paper titled "Neuromorphic Motor Policy Transfer via Spiking Diffusion Models" that she'd been annotating for the past four hours.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** She was twenty-three. Second-year Master's student. Robotics and Autonomous Systems track. Her undergraduate thesis on multi-agent swarm coordination had been cited forty-one times before she'd even graduated — a fact her advisor, Professor Liang, mentioned at every faculty meeting with barely concealed smugness.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** She had the kind of face that made people do a double-take: sharp, precise features, dark eyes that always seemed to be running calculations behind them, and an expression of calm focus that rarely cracked. Her black hair was pulled back in a low ponytail, and she wore her usual uniform — an oversized grey hoodie, black jeans, and wire-frame glasses she didn't technically need anymore since getting LASIK last year but kept wearing because "they help me think."</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** (typing, not looking up) "If you're going to stand behind me breathing on my neck, Lin Yao, you could at least bring coffee."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** (holding two cups of coffee, offended) "I literally AM holding coffee. I've been holding coffee for thirty seconds. You didn't even turn around."</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Lin Yao — twenty-four, third-year PhD student in the same lab, specializing in soft robotics and bio-inspired actuators. She was tall, athletic, perpetually tanned from weekend rock climbing, with a mess of curly hair held back by a pair of lab safety goggles she'd pushed onto her forehead so many times they'd become a permanent accessory. She had the energy of someone who'd had four espressos and zero regrets.</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** (accepting the coffee without turning around) "I could hear the ice rattling. You always get iced Americano from the fourth-floor vending machine when you're procrastinating on your own paper."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** (sitting down in the adjacent chair, legs crossed) "I'm not procrastinating. I'm strategically delaying. There's a difference." (sips coffee) "Also, my finite element sim has been running for six hours and it's only at thirty-seven percent. I literally cannot do anything."</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "You could read a paper."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** "I could also eat glass. Similar emotional experience."</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** The lab door swung open. A third figure walked in — shorter, rounder, wearing a violently orange puffer jacket over her lab coat and carrying a laptop under one arm.</font>

<font color="#D4A534">**He Zhiyu:** "Good news: I just got the motion capture data from the biomechanics lab. Bad news: someone labeled every single marker as 'Point_1' through 'Point_47' with no anatomical reference. I want to commit a crime."</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** He Zhiyu — twenty-two, first-year Master's, human-robot interaction track. She had a round, expressive face, short-cropped hair dyed a subtle auburn, and the disposition of a golden retriever that had just been told the treat jar was empty. Her research focused on intuitive gesture interfaces for assistive robots — work that required the patience of a saint, which she absolutely did not have.</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** "Which crime specifically?"</font>

<font color="#D4A534">**He Zhiyu:** "Arson. The biomechanics lab. Insurance covers it probably."</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "Write a script to re-label based on spatial clustering. It'll take twenty minutes."</font>

<font color="#D4A534">**He Zhiyu:** (stares at Xin) "…Why do you always make it sound so easy? It makes me feel like I have a negative IQ."</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "I can write it for you. Send me the data."</font>

<font color="#D4A534">**He Zhiyu:** (already airdropping the file) "You are a god among mortals and I will name my firstborn after you."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** "Your firstborn will be named Xin?"</font>

<font color="#D4A534">**He Zhiyu:** "Xin He. Sounds powerful. Like a CEO. Or a Bond villain."</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** (a faint smile, eyes still on-screen) "I'd prefer Bond villain."</font>

---

<font color="#808080">**Description:** 1:20 AM. Lin Yao had fallen asleep on her keyboard. He Zhiyu had gone home an hour ago, profusely thanking Xin for the re-labeling script (which had, in fact, taken fourteen minutes). The campus was silent except for the distant hum of the cooling systems in the server farm beneath Building Three.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Xin stretched, rotated her neck until it cracked twice, and pushed back from her desk. The simulation results were good — the quadruped's gait stability had improved by eighteen percent after she'd tweaked the reward function in the reinforcement learning pipeline. She'd write it up tomorrow.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** She was about to shake Lin Yao awake when something caught her eye.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** On the windowsill — the wide ledge of the floor-to-ceiling window behind her workstation — sat an object that had not been there ten minutes ago.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** It was a book. Or rather, a scroll — no, something in between. A thin, leather-bound volume the size of a standard notebook, its cover the color of aged ivory. There was no title on the front. No author's name. Only a single character embossed in gold at the center, rendered in a calligraphic style so ancient it took Xin a full three seconds to parse:</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** 經 — *Scripture.*</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** The window was closed. The lab door had been locked since He Zhiyu left. The security camera in the corner — Xin glanced at it — showed a solid green light, recording normally. There was no rational mechanism by which this object could have arrived.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Xin did not panic. She did not gasp, or stumble backward, or call out to the sleeping Lin Yao. She stood perfectly still for five seconds, then reached for the book with the same calm she used to handle a hot soldering iron — deliberate, controlled, unhurried.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** The moment her fingertips touched the ivory cover, warmth flooded through her hand and up her arm — not painful, not electric, but deep and slow, like pressing her palm against sun-warmed stone. And with the warmth came information.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Not text. Not sound. A direct cognitive transfer — as if the knowledge had always been in her brain and someone had simply flipped the access switch.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** *Scripture of Eternal Life (长生经). Origin: the Immortal Dao Universe, Ninth Heaven Celestial Archive. A foundational body-refinement and qi-cultivation manual designed for mortal-origin practitioners. Cultivates vitality from the molecular level upward — reinforcing cellular structure, enhancing neural conductivity, sharpening organ function, and incrementally expanding lifespan. At advanced stages, the practitioner's physical and cognitive capabilities exceed biological limits by orders of magnitude.*</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** *Stage One: Flesh Tempering — systematic reinforcement of musculature, bone density, and organ resilience through guided internal energy cycling. Expected duration for a practitioner with no prior cultivation base: 3-6 months to first threshold.*</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** *Side effects of early-stage cultivation: enhanced memory retention, accelerated pattern recognition, reduced sleep requirement, heightened sensory acuity.*</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Xin stared at the book for a very long time.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** The scientist in her — the part that had been trained since childhood to think in hypotheses and null results — screamed that this was impossible. Object teleportation violated conservation of energy. "Qi cultivation" was pre-scientific metaphysics. Direct cognitive data transfer implied a mechanism that bypassed every known information-processing pathway in the human brain.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** But the data was there. Clear, structured, internally consistent. The cultivation method described precise breathing patterns, body postures, and mental visualization sequences. It was not vague mystic poetry — it read more like a bioengineering protocol, specifying exact muscle groups, joint angles, and respiratory rhythms with a precision that would have satisfied a peer reviewer.</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** (whispering) "…Either I've developed acute psychosis from sleep deprivation, or the universe just handed me the most interesting dataset I've ever encountered."</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** She weighed her options. Then she did what any good researcher would do with an anomalous observation: she decided to run an experiment.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** She sat cross-legged on the floor of the lab, the Scripture of Eternal Life open across her knees. She followed the first breathing sequence exactly as described — four counts in through the nose, hold for seven, out through the mouth for eight, with her awareness directed along a specific pathway the manual called the "central meridian" — from the crown of her head, down through the spine, to a point three centimeters below her navel.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** For the first two minutes, nothing happened. She was very aware of how absurd she looked — a robotics graduate student sitting on the cold tile floor of a university lab at 1:30 AM, breathing like a yoga instructor and staring at a mysterious book.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Then, on the ninth cycle of breathing, she felt it.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** A warmth. Faint. Deep inside her abdomen — beneath the muscles, beneath the organs, in a place she'd never felt sensation before. Like a pilot light had been ignited in a furnace she didn't know she had. The warmth spread slowly — into her lower spine, up through her ribcage, along the long muscles of her back.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** It lasted approximately four minutes before fading. When it did, Xin opened her eyes and immediately conducted a self-assessment: pulse — normal, slightly elevated. Body temperature — subjectively warmer, no fever. Cognitive state — clear. Unusually clear. Like someone had cleaned a window she'd been looking through her entire life without realizing it was dirty.</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** (looking at her own hands, flexing her fingers slowly) "…Interesting."</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** She closed the book carefully, tucked it into her backpack between her laptop and a crumpled bag of dried plums, and stood up. Then she walked over to Lin Yao and tapped her shoulder.</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "Wake up. Your keyboard left an imprint on your face."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** (jolting awake, wiping drool) "Wh— I wasn't sleeping. I was conducting a human factors study on the ergonomics of desk-based rest." (touches her cheek, feels the key imprints) "…Oh no."</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "Q, W, E, R. It says QWER on your cheek."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** "Like a League of Legends skill bar. At least it's thematically relevant to my life choices." (stands up, groaning) "What time is it?"</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "One thirty. Go home."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** (packing her bag, yawning) "You're leaving too, right? Please tell me you're not going to stay until four again."</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "I'm leaving."</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** They walked together through the empty campus, breath fogging in the cold autumn air. The artificial lake reflected the lights of the research towers — twelve pillars of blue-white luminescence standing against the smoggy sky like digital monoliths.</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** (hugging herself against the cold) "Hey, random question. Do you ever think about what it would be like if all the wuxia stuff was real? Like, actual qi cultivation, internal energy, flying on swords?"</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Xin's backpack suddenly felt very heavy.</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** (neutral tone) "That's a very specific random question."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** "I was reading a web novel during my sim's loading screen. The protagonist discovers some ancient cultivation manual and becomes a superhuman. Classic premise." (laughs) "Could you imagine? Like, 'Sorry, Professor, I can't submit my thesis on time because I was too busy achieving immortality.'"</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "Professor Liang would still dock points for late submission."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** (cackling) "He absolutely would. 'Miss Xin, I don't care if you can split mountains with your palm, the deadline was Friday.'"</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Xin allowed herself a small laugh. Inside her bag, the ivory book was warm against her back — warm in a way that a leather-bound book sitting in a climate-controlled lab had no business being.</font>

---

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Two weeks later.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Xin had practiced the first breathing sequence of the Scripture of Eternal Life every night, without exception. Thirty minutes before bed, sitting on the floor of her single-occupancy graduate dormitory, door locked, curtains drawn.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** The changes were subtle but measurable — and she measured everything.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** She had started logging data from Day One in a private, encrypted spreadsheet titled "Personal Biometric Experiment — Sleep Optimization Study" (a plausible cover story in case anyone ever accessed the file). The metrics she tracked: sleep duration and quality, resting heart rate, grip strength (using a digital dynamometer she'd borrowed from the biomechanics lab), reaction time (via a standardized online test), working memory span (N-back task), and a subjective cognitive clarity score on a 1-10 scale.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** The trends were undeniable.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Sleep requirement: dropped from 7 hours to 5.5 hours with no decrease in daytime alertness. Resting heart rate: from 68 bpm to 59 bpm. Grip strength: from 34 kg to 41 kg — a 20% increase in two weeks, which was physiologically absurd without intensive resistance training. Reaction time: from 247 ms to 198 ms. Working memory N-back score: improved by two levels. Subjective clarity: she'd stopped scoring it after Day Eight because it had been a consistent 10 and the scale was no longer useful.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Most striking of all: she could read faster. Not slightly faster — meaningfully faster. A paper that would have taken her forty minutes to dissect now took twenty-five. She retained more. She saw connections between ideas more quickly. It was as if the scaffolding of her cognition had been quietly rebuilt with better materials while she wasn't looking.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** This morning, sitting in the weekly lab group meeting, she'd noticed something else. Professor Liang was presenting a new locomotion planning framework — a hierarchical model predictive control approach for legged robots on deformable terrain. The math was dense, involving Lie group dynamics and contact-implicit optimization. Normally, she'd follow the general structure in real-time and work through the details afterward.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Today, she followed every line as it appeared. Including two errors in the derivation that Professor Liang had apparently not caught.</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** (raising her hand, politely) "Professor Liang — on slide fourteen, the Jacobian transpose should be the body-frame Jacobian, not the spatial Jacobian. The contact wrench is expressed in the body frame three slides earlier, and the frame wasn't transformed. Also, on slide seventeen, the terminal cost's Hessian is missing the cross-term from the angular velocity coupling."</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** The lab fell silent. Professor Liang — a fifty-seven-year-old man who had published in Nature Robotics three times and served on the editorial board of IO-RAM — stared at slide fourteen for nine seconds, then at slide seventeen for another twelve. Then he looked at Xin over the rim of his glasses.</font>

**Professor Liang:** "…You're right. Both times." (long pause) "I've been using this slide deck for two semesters. Nobody has caught those." (another pause) "Did you pre-read the paper this is based on?"

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "No. I just followed the derivation in real-time."</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Professor Liang took off his glasses, polished them slowly, and put them back on. This was his known tell for being simultaneously impressed and mildly unsettled.</font>

**Professor Liang:** "See me after the meeting."

<font color="#808080">**Description:** After the meeting, he asked her if she'd be interested in co-authoring a paper on contact-implicit whole-body planning. She said yes. He nodded once, said "Good," and went back to polishing his glasses.</font>

---

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Lunch. The third-floor cafeteria of Tower Seven. Xin, Lin Yao, and He Zhiyu occupied their usual table by the window — the one with the slightly wobbly leg that nobody else wanted.</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** (pointing chopsticks at Xin) "Okay, be honest. Are you taking nootropics?"</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "No."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** "Brain-computer interface? Deep-TMS? Some kind of experimental cortical stimulation thing from the neuroscience building?"</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "No."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** "Then explain to me how you just casually corrected Professor Liang's Lie group math in real-time like you were proofreading a grocery list."</font>

<font color="#D4A534">**He Zhiyu:** "She was always like this. She's just being more obvious about it now."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** "She was not always like this. I've known her for two years. She was always smart — freakishly smart — but this is different. This is like watching someone play a video game on a faster processor. Same person, higher frame rate."</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Xin ate her braised eggplant in silence, considering how much to disclose. The answer, for now, was: nothing.</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "I've been sleeping better lately. And exercising more. That's it."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** (squinting) "Exercising. You. The woman who once referred to the gym as 'a building full of people voluntarily accelerating their joint degradation.'"</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "I've revised my position."</font>

<font color="#D4A534">**He Zhiyu:** (leaning in conspiratorially) "Maybe she's in love. I read that being in love increases dopamine and norepinephrine, which enhances focus and—"</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "I am not in love."</font>

<font color="#D4A534">**He Zhiyu:** "That's exactly what someone in love would say."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** "Who is it? Is it someone in our lab? Is it that visiting scholar from ETH Zurich? He DOES have very nice forearms."</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** "This conversation has departed from any useful trajectory."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** "She's deflecting! Classic Xin deflection maneuver — use words that sound like a journal abstract to shut down personal questions."</font>

<font color="#D4A534">**He Zhiyu:** (giggling) "Xin, just admit you're a robot. It would explain so much. You found a firmware update."</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** (the faintest trace of a smile) "If I were a robot, I would be significantly better at social interaction."</font>

<font color="#C76B98">**Lin Yao:** "Debatable. You'd probably just be a robot that's also bad at social interaction."</font>

<font color="#D4A534">**He Zhiyu:** "An asocial robot. That's literally her thesis topic. She's building herself."</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** All three of them laughed — He Zhiyu loudly, Lin Yao with a snort she tried to suppress, and Xin with a quiet exhale through her nose that, for her, constituted raucous laughter.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Beneath the table, Xin flexed her right hand. The grip strength increase wasn't just a number on a spreadsheet — she could feel it. Her fingers moved with a crispness and precision that felt *engineered*. When she'd typed code this morning, she'd noticed her keystroke accuracy was perfect. Not almost perfect. Perfect. Zero typos across four hundred lines.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** The Scripture of Eternal Life lay in her backpack, warm and patient, waiting for tonight's session.</font>

---

<font color="#808080">**Description:** That evening. Graduate dormitory building, Room 417.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Xin sat on the floor again, legs crossed, the Scripture open before her. She had progressed to the third breathing sequence — a more complex pattern that involved simultaneous awareness of three "energy nodes" the manual identified along the spine. In anatomical terms, they corresponded roughly to the lumbar plexus, the cardiac plexus, and the cervical ganglion. The manual didn't use those terms, of course — it called them "Lower Furnace," "Middle Furnace," and "Upper Furnace." But the spatial correspondence was precise enough that Xin suspected whoever had written this understood human neuroanatomy at a level that rivaled modern textbooks.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** She began the breathing cycle. Four in, seven hold, eight out. Awareness flowing down the central meridian like a slow current of warm water.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** On the sixth cycle, the warmth returned — stronger than before. It gathered in her lower abdomen, pulsed once, twice, and then began to radiate outward through pathways that she was increasingly convinced corresponded to major nerve trunks and fascial planes. She could feel it threading through her quadriceps, her deltoids, the intricate musculature of her hands.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** And then, for the first time, it reached her brain.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** The sensation was extraordinary. A slow wave of clarity and heat, starting from the base of her skull, rolling forward through her temporal lobes, her parietal cortex, her prefrontal cortex — she could feel the anatomical geography of it. Her vision sharpened. Her hearing sharpened. The hum of the dormitory's HVAC system, previously background noise, became a distinct signal she could track through three harmonics.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** She opened her eyes. The room looked different — not brighter, but *higher resolution*. She could see the grain of the plywood desk across the room. She could see individual dust particles suspended in the air near the ceiling lamp, frozen in Brownian dance.</font>

<font color="#4A90D9">**Xin:** (very quietly) "…Stage One, first threshold."</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** She stayed very still for a full minute, cataloguing sensations. Then she reached for her phone, opened the encrypted spreadsheet, and began entering data. Her fingers moved across the screen with a speed and precision that startled even her.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Tomorrow, she would run the full battery of tests again. She already knew what the numbers would show — she could feel the answers in her body, in the sharpened architecture of her own nervous system.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** But she would measure anyway. Because that's what scientists do.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** Xin closed the Scripture, set it on her nightstand, and lay down in bed. She was asleep in ninety seconds — deep, dreamless, efficient. Her body hummed faintly with a warmth that had no biological explanation, quietly rebuilding itself from the inside out.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** In two months, she would be measurably the strongest person on campus. In six months, her cognitive test results would break the ceiling of every standardized metric. In a year, three of her papers would be under review at top-tier venues simultaneously.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** But that was all still to come. Tonight, she was just a graduate student with a mysterious book, sleeping five and a half hours, dreaming of nothing.</font>

<font color="#808080">**Description:** The Scripture of Eternal Life glowed faintly on the nightstand — a warmth no thermometer could measure — and waited.</font>
